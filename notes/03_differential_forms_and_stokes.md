## 0. Standing Definitions

$$
V \quad \text{a real vector space}, \qquad \dim V = n, \qquad V^* = \operatorname{Hom}(V,\mathbb{R}).
$$

$$
\Lambda^k(V^*) \quad \text{alternating $k$-linear maps } V\times\cdots\times V \to \mathbb{R} \ (k \text{ factors}), \qquad \Lambda^0(V^*):=\mathbb{R}.
$$

$$
M \quad \text{smooth $n$-manifold (possibly with boundary)}, \qquad p\in M, \qquad \Omega^k(M) \quad \text{smooth $k$-forms on } M.
$$

$$
\Omega_c^k(\mathbb{R}^n) \quad \text{compactly supported smooth $k$-forms on } \mathbb{R}^n \quad \text{(test forms; used again in `notes/08`)}.
$$

## 1. Alternating Multilinear Forms and the Wedge Product

**Definition 1.1.** $\omega \in \Lambda^k(V^*)$ is a map
$\omega : V^k \to \mathbb{R}$, $k$-linear, satisfying
$\omega(v_{\sigma(1)},\dots,v_{\sigma(k)}) = \operatorname{sgn}(\sigma)\,
\omega(v_1,\dots,v_k)$ for every permutation $\sigma \in S_k$.

**Proposition 1.2.** $\dim \Lambda^k(V^*) = \binom{n}{k}$; a basis is given by
$\{e^{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge e^{i_k} : 1\le i_1<\cdots<i_k\le n\}$ for a dual
basis $\{e^1,\dots,e^n\}$ of $V^*$, where $\wedge$ is defined below.

**Definition 1.3 (wedge product).** For $\omega\in\Lambda^k(V^*)$, $\eta \in
\Lambda^\ell(V^*)$,

$$
(\omega\wedge\eta)(v_1,\dots,v_{k+\ell}) := \frac{1}{k!\,\ell!} \sum_{\sigma\in S_{k+\ell}} \operatorname{sgn}(\sigma)\, \omega(v_{\sigma(1)},\dots,v_{\sigma(k)})\, \eta(v_{\sigma(k+1)},\dots,v_{\sigma(k+\ell)}),
$$

giving $\wedge : \Lambda^k(V^*)\times\Lambda^\ell(V^*) \to \Lambda^{k+\ell}(V^*)$.

**Proposition 1.4 (properties of $\wedge$).** $\wedge$ is bilinear,
associative, and graded-commutative: $\omega\wedge\eta =
(-1)^{k\ell}\,\eta\wedge\omega$. In particular for 1-forms $\alpha,\beta \in
\Lambda^1(V^*) = V^*$: $\alpha\wedge\beta = -\beta\wedge\alpha$, so
$\alpha\wedge\alpha = 0$; in coordinates $dx^i\wedge dx^j = -dx^j\wedge dx^i$.

*(Standard multilinear algebra; e.g. Lee, ISM, Ch. 14, or Spivak, CoM, Ch. 4.
Proof by direct verification of the defining sum against a transposition,
then induction on $S_{k+\ell}$ generated by transpositions.)*

**Definition 1.5 ($k$-form on $M$).** A smooth $k$-form is an assignment
$p \mapsto \omega_p \in \Lambda^k(T_p^*M)$, smooth in the sense that in any
chart, $\omega = \omega_{i_1\cdots i_k}\,dx^{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^{i_k}$
(sum over increasing multi-indices) with $\omega_{i_1\cdots i_k} \in
C^\infty(U)$. Write $\Omega^k(M)$ for the space of such $\omega$;
$\Omega^0(M) = C^\infty(M)$; $\Omega^k(M) = 0$ for $k>n=\dim M$ since
$\Lambda^k(T_p^*M)=0$ for $k>n$ by Proposition 1.2.

## 2. Exterior Derivative and the Anchor Proof $d^2=0$

**Definition 2.1 (exterior derivative in a chart).** For $\omega =
\omega_{i_1\cdots i_k}\,dx^{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^{i_k} \in \Omega^k(M)$
(sum over increasing multi-indices $i_1<\cdots<i_k$), define

$$
d\omega := \sum_{i_1<\cdots<i_k} d\omega_{i_1\cdots i_k} \wedge dx^{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^{i_k}, \qquad d\omega_{i_1\cdots i_k} = \frac{\partial \omega_{i_1\cdots i_k}}{\partial x^j}\,dx^j \in \Omega^1(U),
$$

giving $d : \Omega^k(M) \to \Omega^{k+1}(M)$.

**Proposition 2.2 (well-definedness).** $d\omega$ computed in one chart
agrees, after change of coordinates, with $d\omega$ computed in another —
i.e. $d$ does not depend on the chart used to define it. *(Lee, ISM, Thm
14.24; a direct but notationally heavy chain-rule computation, black-boxed
here per `01` §17's guidance on not re-deriving standard machinery every
time it is used — the essential content is the same chain-rule symmetry used
below.)*

**Proposition 2.3 (properties of $d$).** $d$ is $\mathbb{R}$-linear;
$d(f) = df$ (§02 Def. 5.2, consistent notation) for $f\in\Omega^0(M)$; and $d$
satisfies the graded Leibniz rule

$$
d(\omega\wedge\eta) = d\omega\wedge\eta + (-1)^k\,\omega\wedge d\eta, \qquad \omega\in\Omega^k(M),\ \eta\in\Omega^\ell(M).
$$

### Anchor Theorem: $d^2 = 0$

**Theorem 2.4.** For every $\omega \in \Omega^k(M)$, $d(d\omega) = 0$.

*Proof.* By linearity of $d$ it suffices to treat
$\omega = f\,dx^{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^{i_k}$, $f\in C^\infty(U)$, in a
single chart. First treat $k=0$, $\omega = f$:

$$
df = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x^j}\,dx^j
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
d(df) = d\Big(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x^j}\Big)\wedge dx^j = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^i\partial x^j}\,dx^i\wedge dx^j.
$$

Now $\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^i \partial x^j}$ is **symmetric** in
$(i,j)$ (Clairaut/Schwarz theorem on mixed partials, valid since $f\in
C^\infty$; this is the one classical-analysis fact the whole proof rests on),
while $dx^i \wedge dx^j$ is **antisymmetric** in $(i,j)$ by Proposition 1.4.
Summing a symmetric quantity times an antisymmetric quantity over all $(i,j)$
gives zero: relabeling the dummy indices $i\leftrightarrow j$,

$$
\sum_{i,j} \frac{\partial^2f}{\partial x^i\partial x^j}\,dx^i\wedge dx^j
= \sum_{i,j} \frac{\partial^2f}{\partial x^j\partial x^i}\,dx^j\wedge dx^i
= -\sum_{i,j} \frac{\partial^2f}{\partial x^i\partial x^j}\,dx^i\wedge dx^j,
$$

using symmetry of the partials on the middle equality and antisymmetry of
$\wedge$ on the last. A quantity equal to its own negative is $0$, so
$d(df)=0$.

For general $k$, $\omega = f\,dx^{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^{i_k}$:

$$
d\omega = df\wedge dx^{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^{i_k}
$$

(the $dx^{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^{i_k}$ factor has constant, hence
$d$-closed, coefficient $1$, so only the $f$ factor contributes, by the
Leibniz rule Proposition 2.3 with the second term vanishing). Applying $d$
again and the graded Leibniz rule with $k=1$:

$$
d(d\omega) = d(df)\wedge dx^{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^{i_k} - df\wedge d(dx^{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^{i_k}).
$$

The first term is $0$ by the $k=0$ case just proved. The second term: each
$dx^{i_\ell}$ has constant coefficient $1$, so
$d(dx^{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^{i_k}) = 0$ by repeated application of the
Leibniz rule to constant-coefficient factors ($d(1) = 0$). Hence $d(d\omega) =
0$. By well-definedness (Proposition 2.2) this holds independent of chart,
and by linearity it holds for every $\omega\in\Omega^k(M)$. $\blacksquare$

*(Cf. Lee, ISM, Thm 14.31 for the coordinate-free version of the same
argument; Spivak, CoM, Thm 4-11.)*

> **Why this is the single identity behind vector calculus.** Under the
> identifications of `00` §12.4, $d:\Omega^0\to\Omega^1$ is $\nabla$,
> $d:\Omega^1\to\Omega^2$ is $\nabla\times$, $d:\Omega^2\to\Omega^3$ is
> $\nabla\cdot$. Theorem 2.4 applied at $k=0$ gives $\nabla\times(\nabla f)=0$;
> applied at $k=1$ gives $\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times F)=0$. Both classical
> vector identities are the *same* proof — symmetric mixed partials killed by
> an antisymmetric wedge — appearing at two different degrees.

## 3. Orientation, Boundary, Integration

**Definition 3.1 (orientation).** An orientation of $V$ ($\dim V=n$) is a
choice of equivalence class of ordered bases under "related by a
positive-determinant change of basis." An orientation of $M$ is a smooth,
consistent choice of orientation of $T_pM$ for every $p$ (equivalently, an
atlas all of whose transition maps have positive-determinant Jacobian).

**Definition 3.2 (manifold with boundary, and its induced orientation).** A
smooth $n$-manifold with boundary is modeled locally on the half-space
$\mathbb{H}^n = \{x\in\mathbb{R}^n : x^n \ge 0\}$; its boundary $\partial M$
is the set of points mapping to $\{x^n=0\}$ under some chart, and is a smooth
$(n-1)$-manifold. If $M$ is oriented, $\partial M$ receives the
**outward-normal-first** induced orientation: a basis
$(w_1,\dots,w_{n-1})$ of $T_p(\partial M)$ is positive iff
$(\nu,w_1,\dots,w_{n-1})$ is a positive basis of $T_pM$, where $\nu$ points
outward.

**Definition 3.3 (integral of a top-degree form).** For $\omega \in
\Omega^n(M)$ with compact support contained in a single oriented chart
$(U,\varphi)$, write $\omega = f\,dx^1\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^n$ in that chart
and define

$$
\int_M \omega := \int_{\varphi(U)} f\circ\varphi^{-1}\ dx^1\cdots dx^n \quad \text{(ordinary Lebesgue/Riemann integral on } \mathbb{R}^n\text{)}.
$$

For general $\omega$, extend by a partition of unity subordinate to an
oriented atlas (standard; black-boxed per `01` §17). **Proposition 3.4** this
is independent of the chart used, precisely because the change-of-variables
formula supplies the factor $|\det J| = \det J$ (positive by orientation
compatibility) needed to match $\int_{\varphi(U)} f\circ\varphi^{-1}$ computed
in two different oriented charts.

> **Type-check — the degree-matching rule.** $\int_M \omega$ is defined only
> for $\omega \in \Omega^n(M)$, $n = \dim M$: a $k$-form integrates over a
> $k$-dimensional oriented object, never a mismatched dimension. This is why
> "$\int_S \omega$" for $S\subseteq M$ oriented $k$-dimensional and $\omega
> \in \Omega^k(M)$ is computed by first restricting/pulling back $\omega$ to
> $S$ (via the inclusion or a parametrization) to get a top-degree form *on*
> $S$, then applying Definition 3.3 to $S$ itself, treated as an
> $k$-manifold in its own right.

## 4. Generalized Stokes' Theorem

### 4.1 Anchor proof: Stokes on the standard cube

We first prove Stokes for $M = [0,1]^k =: I^k$, then explain how the general
case reduces to this via charts and partitions of unity.

**Theorem 4.1 (Stokes on $I^k$).** Let $\omega \in \Omega^{k-1}(\mathbb{R}^k)$
have compact support (in an open set containing $I^k$), $\omega =
\sum_{i=1}^k (-1)^{i-1} f_i\, dx^1\wedge\cdots\wedge \widehat{dx^i}
\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^k$ (hat = omit), $f_i\in C^\infty$. Then

$$
\int_{I^k} d\omega = \int_{\partial I^k} \omega,
$$

with $\partial I^k$ given its induced orientation (Definition 3.2).

*Proof.* By linearity, treat one term at a time: $\omega = f\,dx^1\wedge
\cdots\wedge\widehat{dx^i}\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^k$ for fixed $i$ (dropping
the sign and index bookkeeping of the general case, which is identical). By
Definition 2.1,

$$
d\omega = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x^i}\,dx^i\wedge dx^1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{dx^i}\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^k
= (-1)^{i-1}\,\frac{\partial f}{\partial x^i}\,dx^1\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^k
$$

(moving $dx^i$ past $i-1$ factors to restore increasing order, picking up
$(-1)^{i-1}$ by Proposition 1.4). Hence

$$
\int_{I^k} d\omega = (-1)^{i-1}\int_{I^k}\frac{\partial f}{\partial x^i}\,dx^1\cdots dx^k.
$$

By Fubini, integrate the $x^i$-variable first:

$$
\int_0^1 \frac{\partial f}{\partial x^i}(x^1,\dots,x^k)\,dx^i = f(x^1,\dots,x^{i-1},1,x^{i+1},\dots,x^k) - f(x^1,\dots,x^{i-1},0,x^{i+1},\dots,x^k)
$$

by the 1-variable Fundamental Theorem of Calculus. So

$$
\int_{I^k} d\omega = (-1)^{i-1}\left[\int_{\{x^i=1\}} f\,dx^1\cdots\widehat{dx^i}\cdots dx^k - \int_{\{x^i=0\}} f\,dx^1\cdots\widehat{dx^i}\cdots dx^k\right].
$$

The boundary $\partial I^k$ consists of the $2k$ facets $\{x^i=0\}$,
$\{x^i=1\}$, $i=1,\dots,k$. On every facet other than $\{x^i=0\}$,
$\{x^i=1\}$, the restriction of $\omega = f\,dx^1\wedge\cdots\wedge
\widehat{dx^i}\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^k$ to that facet is $0$: the facet
$\{x^m = c\}$ ($m\ne i$) forces one of the remaining coordinate differentials
in $\omega$'s wedge to restrict to $0$ (since $x^m$ is constant along that
facet, $dx^m|_{\text{facet}}=0$). So $\int_{\partial I^k}\omega$ reduces to
exactly the two facets computed above, and one checks (by unwinding
Definition 3.2's outward-normal convention: outward normal on $\{x^i=1\}$ is
$+\partial_i$, on $\{x^i=0\}$ is $-\partial_i$) that the induced orientation
supplies exactly the sign pattern $(-1)^{i-1}[\,\cdot_{x^i=1} -
\cdot_{x^i=0}\,]$ matched above. Hence $\int_{I^k}d\omega =
\int_{\partial I^k}\omega$ for this term, and by linearity for general
$\omega$. $\blacksquare$

*(This is the standard cube proof; cf. Spivak, CoM, Thm 4-9; Lee, ISM, proof
of Thm 16.11 reduces the general manifold case to exactly this computation.)*

### 4.2 General Stokes

**Theorem 4.2 (Generalized Stokes, general case).** Let $M$ be a smooth,
oriented $k$-manifold with boundary $\partial M$ (induced orientation), and
$\omega \in \Omega^{k-1}(M)$ compactly supported. Then

$$
\boxed{\int_M d\omega = \int_{\partial M} \omega.}
$$

*Proof sketch (depth B — reduction to Theorem 4.1, not reproved at depth C
here since it is a bookkeeping reduction, not new mathematical content).*
Cover $\operatorname{supp}(\omega)$ by finitely many oriented charts, each
identifying a piece of $M$ with an open subset of $\mathbb{R}^k$ or
$\mathbb{H}^k$; take a subordinate partition of unity $\{\rho_\alpha\}$ so
$\omega = \sum_\alpha \rho_\alpha\omega$. In each chart, after pulling back by
the (orientation-preserving) chart map, $\rho_\alpha\omega$ becomes a
compactly-supported form on $\mathbb{R}^k$ or $\mathbb{H}^k$, to which Theorem
4.1 applies verbatim (the half-space case is identical, since the boundary
contribution from the interior facets vanishes exactly as in §4.1, leaving
only the $\{x^k=0\}$ facet). Summing over $\alpha$ and using $d(\sum_\alpha
\rho_\alpha) = d(1) = 0$ to handle cross terms from the Leibniz rule applied
to $d(\rho_\alpha\omega)$ recovers the full statement. *(Lee, ISM, Thm 16.11,
full details.)* $\blacksquare$

### 4.3 Special cases as typed corollaries

Each classical theorem is Theorem 4.2 with $(M,\partial M,\omega)$ specialized
as follows; each line names the exact types being matched.

**Corollary 4.3 (Fundamental Theorem of Calculus).** $M = [a,b]$ (a
1-manifold with boundary $\{a,b\}$, oriented $b\to a$ negative to $a\to b$
positive), $\omega = f \in \Omega^0([a,b]) = C^\infty([a,b])$, $d\omega = f'\,dx$:

$$
\int_a^b f'(x)\,dx = f(b) - f(a).
$$

**Corollary 4.4 (Green's theorem).** $M = D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$ a compact
region with boundary curve $\partial D$ (positively/counterclockwise
oriented), $\omega = P\,dx+Q\,dy \in \Omega^1(D)$, $d\omega = (Q_x-P_y)\,dx
\wedge dy$:

$$
\oint_{\partial D} P\,dx+Q\,dy = \iint_D (Q_x-P_y)\,dA.
$$

**Corollary 4.5 (Classical Stokes' theorem).** $M = S \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3$
an oriented surface with boundary curve $\partial S$, $\omega = P\,dx+Q\,dy+
R\,dz \in \Omega^1(S)$, $d\omega$ as in `00` §12.2 (identified with $\nabla
\times F$, $F=(P,Q,R)$, via the Euclidean metric and orientation, `notes/04`
§1.1's musical isomorphism):

$$
\oint_{\partial S} F\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_S (\nabla\times F)\cdot d\mathbf{S}.
$$

**Corollary 4.6 (Divergence theorem).** $M = \Omega\subseteq\mathbb{R}^3$ a
compact solid region with boundary surface $\partial\Omega$ (outward
orientation), $\eta = P\,dy\wedge dz+Q\,dz\wedge dx+R\,dx\wedge dy \in
\Omega^2(\Omega)$, $d\eta = (\nabla\cdot F)\,dx\wedge dy\wedge dz$ as in `00`
§12.3:

$$
\iiint_\Omega \nabla\cdot F\,dV = \oiint_{\partial\Omega} F\cdot d\mathbf{S}.
$$

> Each corollary follows from Theorem 4.2 purely by (a) checking $M$,
> $\partial M$ have the stated dimension and orientation, (b) substituting the
> stated $\omega$, and (c) invoking the low-degree $d$-formulas of `00`
> §12.1–12.3. No new analysis is needed beyond Theorem 4.2 itself — this is
> the precise sense in which "Green/Stokes/Gauss are shadows of one theorem"
> (`00` §16.1, `01` §5.4).

## 5. Pullback Commutes with $d$

**Proposition 5.1.** For $F : M \to N$ smooth and $\omega \in \Omega^k(N)$,
$F^*(d\omega) = d(F^*\omega)$.

*(Lee, ISM, Thm 14.26; direct coordinate computation using the chain rule,
of the same flavor as Proposition 2.2.)* This is what makes the pullback
integral identity of `00` §13, $\int_{X(\Omega)}\omega = \int_\Omega
X^*\omega$, compatible with Stokes: pulling back before or after applying $d$
gives the same answer, so Stokes' theorem transports correctly along smooth
parametrizations and, later (`notes/07` §3), along Lipschitz ones via the
area formula.
